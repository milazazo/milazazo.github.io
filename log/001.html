making of/thoughts
Best viewed on desktop. Originally posted 15/10/21.

Welcome to my page on the "making of and thoughts" on producing my first dojinshi. Maybe it isn't an appropriate time to post it yet, considering that I haven't recieved the books in person, but I want to share how I've handled it so far anyway. As well, I want to cover my thoughts on producing dojinshi and zines as someone who lives in the 'west', specifically as a New Zealander who likes very unpopular characters.

My recently-finished dojinshi, The Fantastic Fireman! is a 40 page comic centered around Shinsoku Ikkon from THE iDOLM@STER SideM. The story is action... mystery... comedy... something. I don't know how to describe it, it's typical SideM stuff with heaps of obscure references and a generous amount of NZ slang. To be honest, I don't think anyone would understand it aside from myself (I am not accepting refunds if you don't understand it, you will simply have to read every Shinsoku event like me).

I started work on it back in 2019, so it's been quite a while, but I swear! I'm quicker at drawing than this! There were some issues... all that and more will be explored in this page. Use the navigation buttons on the side to skip to specific segments!

Dojin Development

I suppose we should start with intention. To be honest, my head was not screwed on straight as a teenager and I had too many meaningless problems. The truth was that I just wanted to make a dojinshi to prove my own worth against others. It's a pretty meaningless competition considering that no one else makes English language SideM dojinshi to begin with. I wasn't even interested in participating in artist alley/conventions at the time so I have no clue how or where I planned to sell it.

I always joked about wanting to draw a Shinsoku yaoi dojin before I turned 18, but when I got started on the actual dojin, I decided that I didn't want to include romantic elements at all. Unfortunately, Shinsoku's relationship is just kinda like that in canon so it comes off as gay anyway. We live in a society. I was also very into superhero comics at the time (thanks to Spiderverse) and so I decided the story would revolve around Shinsoku performing as superheroes. Suzaku would be the hero, of course, but Genbu would have to play the role of the villain... it was a dynamic that hadn't yet been explored in SideM canon at the time. I think that they played those roles in DOS now, but frankly I've been avoiding reading it since it's LOS-related.

Story and Script

When it comes to writing comic scripts, I'm pretty casual about it... I don't bother with labelling panels or pages unless it's meant for someone else's viewing. But since this was such a long story, I prepared it further by writing a detailed script. As well, I researched how DC and Marvel handled script writing. The process is very different from manga as writers tend to differ from artists— not that some manga don't have separate writers and artists, but it's on a whole other scale in the major superhero comics. Check out this link if you want to see how DC scripts are handled. As you can see, it's very detailed, with the writers listing out what goes on in each panel. When they affect the layout this much, they're essentially doing the work of the visual artist as well. I always talk about Tom King's writing style shining through not by his dialogue or plot choices, but by his use of the 3×3 panel page layout.

On the topic of the dojin's story, it was not good at first. And actually, I don't even think it's good now (once again, I am not accepting refunds). There was a lot of forced drama to begin with, caused by misunderstandings between Suzaku and Genbu. And I realized, wait— this story sucks! It's OOC and lame and doesn't have any real weight to it. So I rewrote the entire script so that it would match my view of Shinsoku proper. In the process, the story became more complicated as I inserted an OC antagonist character and action scenes. With the added complexities, I also needed to use my pages more wisely. Because of that, a lot of pages ended up with a ton of dialogue; pretty standard for a superhero comic, but seriously excessive for a non-gag manga.

SideM Mobage's event scenes are pretty brief, but they can pack a lot of story inside. I aimed for something like that; combined with the script style of 70's and 80's comics, it became a very tight story. If I was trying to do it in a regular manga style, the comic would've been well into 100... 120 pages. Somewhere around that range. I think the final product ended up feeling very rushed for that reason, but if Mackenzie says it's okay, then it's okay!

Ultimately, I guess I was trying to create "the ultimate Shinsoku dojinshi". I knew that I would never be able to produce more than one, so the story had to be really good and include everything I loved! It needed to have drama and a cool art style and everything... maybe that's what made it take so long to complete.

Storyboard

I bought Clip Studio Paint EX specifically for drawing this comic since I saw all of the Japanese dojin artists had it too. Frankly I wouldn't bother with it if you don't plan to make any comics longer than 20~ pages though. I never use it when making multi-page comics regularly, just too much of a hassle to set everything up. However, it is still useful for keeping all your files together, 3D binding preview, and mass exporting.

Anyway, onto the storyboard itself... this is something I wouldn't do today, but, um... I didn't actually draw a full storyboard. Like, it was really tiring to draw sketches for what would happen thirty pages into the future, I just wanted to draw pretty pictures of Genbu! But that was a big mistake. It's hard to tell how well a story will work if you don't draw out the proper layout beforehand. It's easy to lose motiviation to work on the storyboard once you work on the fun stuff. I was really stupid!!! I didn't draw a storyboard until March of 2021!

Pencils and Inks

The first thing I have to say, is why the hell did I choose this art style!? It's so tiring! Oh my God!!! And it's Shinsoku Ikkon too, who are the hardest characters to draw ever. I really deserve something amazing for forcing myself to draw Suzaku's hair like forty times. Jesus Christ. Well, like I said before, I was pretty sure this would be the only Shinsoku dojinshi I could ever create, so the art style needed to be amazing! But Dios Mio, I can't... I can't ever draw like this again... it looks so cool though...

The art was the period where I just gave up for a year. It was too hard and the story was too cringe so I didn't want to work on it. Also, because of my interest in superheroes, specifically Daredevil, I got into Christianity and started reading the Bible and got bored of working on it. But I'm good now. When I got back into drawing the dojin, I pushed out the sketches super quickly and simplified the lineart style so that I could handle it better. There's really a drop in quality between the first and second half of the comic... so embarassing.

There was one thing that helped, and that was that I didn't have to draw in the same art style constantly. Like with SideM Mobage marathon events, part of the story takes place as a story-within-a-story; basically, the 'acted' section. Here it was represented by a change in art style to a 70's superhero style. Too bad I suck at drawing like that, though! It was basically an excuse for me to not worry so much about nice looking lineart and screentones. Looked really ugly, LOL.

Screentones and Effects

My bad habits struck again and I did screentones for the first few pages before completing inks, pencils, or even storyboards for the rest. I just wanted to see how it would look like completed! But I really wouldn't reccomend doing that. It's much better to do screentones all at once at the end for both the sake of motivation and consistency.

Not much else to say on the topic of effects except that... well, because the story was so cringe, I avoided looking at the text or script as I drew it. And I didn't realize that there was a bunch of hand-written dialogue in the script until literally the day before I ordered the books. Oops.

Marketing

I decided that I would sell the dojin at my university's art stall in a local convention in October, so I tried to have at least two weeks for pre-orders in September. I wanted to use a store system so I coded my own website and used Ecwid to embed a store. In retrospect, I would've used Google Forms instead; leaving things up to an automated store system just made me feel way too anxious! Since it was an important event, I also paid my friend Trish to translate some information into Japanese. There was only one sale to Japan, but I'm still very happy about that! I did post it on Pixiv, but not on BOOTH. If I have any leftover copies, I might add it there sometime.

In regards to other marketing things, I did not bother at all LMAO. I hate posting on my main account and being 'noisy' so it was really difficult for me to advertise it out loud and often. The other thing was that Shinsoku are not very popular at all, and I felt like I had already gotten to the extent of my reach when the post hit around 90 retweets. I have a lot to say about this later on. But anyway, the main takeaway is that I just could not bother with promoting it further when I knew that everyone who liked Shinsoku would've seen the post anyway.

Aside from the Ecwid store system, my main worry was that I priced it way too high. The convention was originally in late October, and because of COVID, I was worried about shipping times from Australia. Thus, I opted to print from New Zealand instead, which is extremely costly in comparison; the price quotes I got were around $250 NZD for 25 books, while printing from Mixam AU was around $180 NZD for 25 books, including shipping. Anyway, at the time, I was going by the $10 per book pricing, but I also had to account for Paypal fees and GST (I will pay my taxes). The price ended up being inflatedly high for that reason, but there was one more thing: convention at conventions.

Dojinshi aren't common in New Zealand, so there was nothing to compare to on that front, but anime art zines (solo) tend to sell for around $10~$15 for like, 20 pages. I didn't want to undercut, so I decided to sell at $15 NZD, which became the base price. I think that price (plus the fees/GST, resulting in $17.50 for online buyers) was fine in the western world. Seriously, right after I started pre-orders I saw a western fan sell a ⬤⬤⬤ ⬤⬤⬤⬤⬤ comic for $10 USD and it was like 12 pages long. Evidently, people are okay with paying more for fan comics if they like the character. Well, the problem for me was that my comic was stupidly expensive compared to dojinshi in Japan! It's like three times the price of a regular dojin of its size and quality. But I'm going, my God, what can I do!? The production cost alone is $10 NZD and I have to account for international fees, and I don't want to undercut others! In order to justify the price, I also decided to include a sticker and mini-print which were very easy and cheap to produce in comparison.

Still, I regret that I couldn't price it lower. Midway through pre-orders, COVID hit New Zealand again and we were put back into lockdown. Because of that, the convention was postponed to November and I had time to order from Mixam AU which had a cheaper price of around $7 NZD per book. I decided to use the extra money to upgrade the dojin so that it would have a 'soft touch' cover. I also decided to offer and include sketches for anyone who had purchased a copy, just so that $3~ wouldn't feel like it went to waste. It sounds like, really over-the-top to worry about this, but it's really difficult to ignore for me. SideM is a series so ingrained in Japanese fandom that I felt horrible pricing it this much higher than others when the quality was not good enough.

Production

As mentioned before, I decided to print from Mixam AU. The ordering system is very easy to use. The only problem is that... um... they don't actually ship to New Zealand on the site, so I had to contact them manually for days to sort out the situation. It was fine and the staff were nice, but it was just time consuming... mostly my own fault so I can't complain. My advice is that you should definitely check your PDF proof thoroughly, because I ended up having to cancel and reorder at last minute after noticing grammar and art errors.

Post-Development

Here's a summary of advice from the word ramble up top:
  • Plan out the story properly from the beginning; if it's a long dojin, you definitely need to get the dialogue down before you start.
  • Do the entire storyboard first before starting on the drawing.
  • In general, finish out one stage before you move on to the next!
  • Don't pick a difficult art style for your first dojin. I promise you, you can make another one in the future.
  • Also, don't do a long comic for your first dojin. Something around 12~16 pages is fine.
  • Don't do a story you're not comfortable with! I struggled a lot because I found the story to be so cringe that I couldn't even look at it. It would've been much easier if it was written in my usual style.
  • Be sure to properly check the PDF before you submit it!
  • Don't live in an island nation of five million people.

After making this Shinsoku dojinshi, I very quickly produced an Ace Attorney one as well, following these steps. It was significantly shorter (only six pages) and was an adaptation of an old fanfiction, so I finished it in only four days. I put far less effort into how good each individual character/panel looked which also helped me speed through it. Once I was done, I printed it at Warehouse Stationery, a chain copycentre in New Zealand. It was $3.90 NZD for 10 copies pre-bound, so a pretty good deal even if it was just two pieces of paper stapled together. It feels so professional when I hold it in my hand... yass... physical objects.

In the future, I want to try to make more short dojinshi like this. I think that going straight into a long and intense story was too difficult for me, especially when it was centered around characters like Shinsoku who no one else cared about... I just didn't have much motivation to work on it.

That being said, the next dojin I'm working on is literally centered around Korekiyo Shinguji and it's around 30~40 pages long so maybe I've learned nothing.

Dojinshi VS. Zines

I'm fresh off of completing my essay about dojin culture in Touhou fandom (will not be publishing that publicly because it's cringe) so I have a lot of thoughts on this! We're specifically about the decision to produce solo (or small group) black and white comics as opposed to color art book-style zines. I only really complain about this to my friends, but the truth is, I'm not a huge fan of modern zines. It might seem hypocritical when I participate in so many and am even running one at the moment, but, it's not like I hate all of them and think they're inherently bad. I guess I'm not huge on how professionally they take themselves? But when you're dealing with real-world money, I get it, um... so... maybe it's the scope. Or that I just don't think illustration is that interesting and want to see more comics instead. Yeah, that's it.

But, well, there's a reason zines have prevailed over dojinshi-type fanbooks in the west, and I have a couple of ideas as to why:

Marketing

I hear so often, "just make a zine with your friends or by yourself!" but I think that to be honest, it's hard to sell fandom products unleess you're already established in that fandom or if you're a mega popular artist already. Frankly for myself, SideM is the only fandom I would be able to sell merch in. Maybe Ace Attorney. But no matter how good my art is right now, I have no means of selling a product to another fandom that I'm not involved with heavily. I mean, my regular art doesn't get shares, so what makes you think I can suddenly start selling JJBA fanbooks by myself? Friends help, but it doesn't help if the friends aren't involved in the fandom either. They don't have the trustworthiniess of the fans; both trust in that they'll deliver the product and that the art/writing will be of good quality.

Zines don't have this issue for the most part, because there are usually so many different people involved. Even if the mods aren't in the fandom, they'll inevitably get artist applying or guest artists who are, and that can increase the trustworthiniess of the zine. If an artist isn't invovled in the fandom, they'll be able to 'sell' their product in a way that ties into it via other artists. It's like a win-win for everyone. *

I think the other place where this could be avoided is at IRL conventions, where people will buy merch even if they don't know the artist or don't like the piece in particular. But here, the problem is that unless you're doing a popular series, there's no point. You'll sell like, five copies at most. I'm speaking from the perspective of a New Zealander, but even in America there isn't one massive convention like Comiket that everyone goes to... really, what's the chances of you selling a lot of copies of your iM@S zine IRL?

* Okay, I think there's another exception to this: friends are more likely to buy your books/merchandise directly to support you, rather than buying a more expensive zine/zine bundle where they're paying to support a bunch of other people they don't know as well.

Time

Are you gonna waste all that time just to sell a few copies? I can't even count the amount of hours I spent on the Shinsoku dojin, but it must've been 350+. Now, I'm an outlier, I'm slow, but I only sold 18 copies online. Most of which were friends. I do have to take into account that Shinsoku are insanely unpopular even for unpopular characters, but I'm sure there's not many people willing to spend that long on something that nets less than $50 profit.

And drawing comics takes longer than regular illustrations to begin with. Most of the comics in zines are 2~4 pages long, meaning that on top of having to do more work than a regular illustration, artists also have to double or triple that by doing more pages. Most dojinshi are in black and white but still, who would bother with that when you can quickly smash out an illustration in four hours? The fact is that as an artist, you could work on a lot of zines in the span of the time it'd take for you to participate in a dojinshi/anthology. You could either get five contributor bundles for free, or one comic that isn't even in color, and it's up to you how you want to use your time.

Shinsoku and Burnin' Spirit

I feel the need to reiterate this for people who do not know iDOLM@STER: Shinsoku Ikkon are insanely unpopular. I'm saying, they're unpopular to the point where I've either translated or commissioned most ENG translations of their content myself. I don't want it to be this way but they are characters who I so very much am the only person that care about them (well, probably not Suzaku. But Genbu for sure). I've been trying to express this for a long time but I feel like I've wasted my youth on them.

I don't think I've gained anything from being a fan of Shinsoku aside from my friends, but I would've gotten the same friends regardless of which SideM character I liked (seriously, SideM fandom was tiny back in 2016). Actually, I think that I've actively 'lost' because of Shinsoku. Maybe it wasn't an issue before; I was always planning to go into STEM and spent a measly half-year as an engineering major. 'Being good at art' and 'being popular online for art' wouldn't matter there.
